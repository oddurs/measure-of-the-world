## Appendix A: Mathematical Derivations

### Context
Extended derivations that would interrupt narrative flow in the main text. Each derivation should be complete, self-contained, and referenced from the relevant chapter.

### Required Derivations

**A.1 Spherical Trigonometry Essentials**
- The spherical triangle: angles and sides on a sphere
- The spherical law of cosines (both forms)
- The spherical law of sines
- The four-parts formula and Napier's analogies
- Worked example: computing great-circle distance between two points

**A.2 The Lunar Distance Calculation**
- Full worked example from raw observations to longitude
- Clearing the distance: parallax correction derivation
- Clearing the distance: refraction correction derivation
- Interpolation in lunar tables
- Complete numerical example with period-appropriate data

**A.3 Stellar Aberration**
- Velocity addition in the classical limit
- Derivation of aberration angle from observer velocity and speed of light
- Annual aberration ellipse geometry
- Diurnal aberration (smaller effect from Earth's rotation)

**A.4 The Equation of Time**
- Eccentricity contribution: mean anomaly to true anomaly (Kepler's equation)
- Obliquity contribution: projection of ecliptic motion onto equator
- Combining both effects
- Full calculation for arbitrary date

**A.5 Precession and Nutation**
- Lunisolar precession: torque on Earth's equatorial bulge
- Precession rate from moment of inertia and angular momentum
- Nutation: lunar nodal regression forcing
- Principal nutation terms

**A.6 Refraction**
- Atmospheric refraction model (plane-parallel and spherical approximations)
- Refraction integral derivation
- Empirical refraction tables and their use
- Refraction at the horizon: the critical case

### Format
Each derivation:
- States the result to be derived
- Lists assumptions and approximations
- Proceeds step by step with intermediate results
- Provides numerical example where applicable
- Notes historical context (how the derivation was originally performed)

## Appendix D: Visiting Greenwich

**D.3 The National Maritime Museum**
- Navigation galleries
- Harrison's chronometers (H1–H4)
- Instrument collections
- Longitude-related exhibits

**D.4 The Astronomy Centre and Planetarium**
- Modern exhibits
- Planetarium shows

**D.5 The Mathematically Inclined Visitor**
- What to look for: graduation scales, micrometers, adjustment screws
- Questions to ask: how was this calibrated? What was the limiting error source?

- Cambridge: Institute of Astronomy, historical instruments
- Herstmonceux: limited access, but exterior and grounds

### Format
Practical and specific, with attention to what a reader of this book would want to see. Not a general tourist guide but a technical enthusiast's companion.
