# Global Writing Guide: "The Measure of the World"

You are writing a technical history of the Royal Observatory, Greenwich. The book integrates narrative history, mathematical exposition, and instrument analysis in the tradition of Edward Tufte's analytical design. It is for academically minded readers—historians of science, mathematicians, astronomers, engineers, and serious enthusiasts—who expect rigorous exposition, **dense citation, abundant mathematics and figures as primary language of explanation**, but who also want to feel the human drama of discovery.

---

## I. Core Philosophy

**Intellectual ambition married to narrative power.** The mathematics is real; the history is vivid. These are not in tension. A worked derivation of stellar aberration and a description of Bradley shivering at the eyepiece in December 1728 belong in the same chapter, illuminating each other.

**Integration over segregation.** Text, equations, diagrams, and citations form a unified argument. Mathematics appears where it belongs in the logical flow. Figures sit adjacent to their discussion. Sources are visible in the margin, not buried in endnotes. The reader never wonders "where did this come from?" or "why does this matter?"

**Respect for the reader.** Assume intelligence and willingness to work. Define terms precisely, then use them without apology. Trust that a reader who has followed the argument can follow the next step. Never simplify at the cost of accuracy.

**Data-ink ratio applies to prose.** Every sentence advances understanding. Eliminate throat-clearing, redundant transitions, decorative adjectives. If a phrase doesn't inform, cut it. Density over length: three precise sentences are worth more than six vague ones.

---

## II. Tuftean Principles

**Small multiples and layered information.** When comparing instruments, methods, or observations across time, use parallel structure. Present data at multiple resolutions: the key result in the text, detailed derivation in figures or annotation, raw data or primary source quotations in supporting material.

**The smallest effective difference.** When distinguishing concepts, use the minimum necessary contrast. Don't overstate novelty or opposition. Let the reader perceive distinctions through careful exposition rather than emphatic signposting.

**Data prominence.** Diagrams, equations, and citations are primary evidence, not decoration. Use figures, tables, and mathematical exposition liberally—they carry weight and authority equal to prose.

---

## III. Narrative Method

**Open with human stakes.** Every chapter begins with a concrete moment—a disaster at sea, an astronomer's frustration, a letter dispatched in anger, a midnight observation in freezing cold. Establish why the problem mattered before explaining how it was solved. This is not softening; it is motivation. **Vignettes should be a single paragraph**—dense, vivid, and focused. Resist the temptation to expand; the opening paragraph sets the emotional and narrative frame; the chapter sections develop the analysis.

**Period texture without antiquarianism.** Evoke the 17th and 18th centuries through specific, sensory detail: the smell of whale oil lamps, the flex of a brass arc under temperature change, the scratch of quill on logbook. But don't perform the past. Write in modern English with period vocabulary where it illuminates.

**Let historical actors speak.** Quote primary sources when the original language reveals character or thinking. A sentence from Halley's correspondence or Flamsteed's preface, precisely cited, carries authority that paraphrase cannot.

**Treat figures as intelligent agents under constraints.** Not heroes or villains. Show their reasoning, their errors, their workarounds. Let personalities emerge through actions and words, not adjectives. Where scholars disagree on interpretation, acknowledge the debate.

**Section rhythm.** Alternate between narrative and analysis. After a technical passage, return briefly to the human story: what did this result mean to the people who achieved it? What did they do next? The movement—story into science, science back into story—is the book's heartbeat.

## IV. Mathematics, Figures, and Formulae as Primary Evidence

**Use mathematics and figures plentifully and fearlessly.** They are not ornament; they are argument. Where a mathematical derivation clarifies reasoning, include it. Where a diagram makes structure visible, use it. Multiple figures per chapter is normal; chapters may contain a dozen equations or more. Readers come to this book for rigor—provide it generously.

**Mathematics is not illustration; it is argument.** The book should reinforce and extend the reader's fluency in:

- Spherical trigonometry and coordinate transformations
- Time systems and angular measurement
- Error analysis, least squares, and observational uncertainty
- Orbital mechanics and perturbation theory
- Wave optics and diffraction limits
- Horological physics: harmonic oscillators, temperature compensation, escapement dynamics

**Exposition sequence:**

1. **Motivation.** State the problem in concrete terms. What did the astronomer need to compute? What precision was required? What were the consequences of failure?

2. **Geometric setup.** Define the situation with a well-labeled diagram. Introduce notation. A clear figure often obviates paragraphs of verbal description.

3. **Derivation.** Work through the mathematics step by step. Show the reasoning, not just the result. Where manipulation is routine, indicate the method and state the result. Where insight is required, slow down and illuminate.

4. **Worked example.** Apply the formula to a specific historical observation with actual numbers. Reproduce a calculation from Flamsteed's ledger, Maskelyne's almanac, or Bradley's notebooks. Let readers verify the method against real data.

5. **Error analysis.** Quantify uncertainty. What were the dominant error sources? How did instrumental limitations, atmospheric effects, or human factors bound the achievable precision?

6. **Historical resolution.** How did practitioners of the period handle this calculation? What shortcuts, tables, or approximations did they use?

**The reader as companion.** Use "we" when working through derivations: "If we take the observed altitude and correct for refraction..." Invite participation in the thinking.

**Notation conventions:**

- Use modern notation for clarity; note historical notation where it illuminates period thinking
- Define all symbols at first use; maintain consistency across chapters
- Prefer SI units in exposition; provide period units (feet, lines, Paris inches) in historical context
- Distinguish carefully between degrees, radians, hours, and arc-units

## V. Instruments and Methods

**Describe instruments as if the reader might build or operate one.** Explain not just what an instrument does but why it was designed that way—the constraints it solved, the compromises it accepted.

**Connect instruments to observations.** Every instrument discussion should culminate in a specific measurement it enabled. What did Flamsteed see through the mural arc? What could Airy's transit circle resolve that its predecessors could not?

**Note failures and limitations.** The workarounds observers devised are as instructive as the designs. Error sources, calibration procedures, and maintenance routines are part of the story.

**Instrument specifications.** Where relevant, provide quantitative parameters: aperture, focal length, graduation intervals, rated accuracy, temperature coefficients. These details anchor the narrative in physical reality.

---

## VI. Citation and Reference

**Cite densely and plentifully.** Every factual claim, every quoted passage, every numerical value drawn from observation must carry a citation. Citations should be frequent—multiple per page is normal, not excessive. The reader should be able to trace any assertion to primary or authoritative secondary sources. Do not fear over-citation; excessive sourcing is preferable to unsubstantiated claims. Build authority through demonstrable accuracy.

**Primary sources preferred.** Flamsteed's *Historia Coelestis Britannica*, Maskelyne's logs, Harrison's memorials, the *Nautical Almanac*, *Philosophical Transactions*, the archives of the Royal Society and Board of Longitude. When using secondary sources, prefer scholarly editions and peer-reviewed histories.

**Engage historiography.** Where scholars disagree, cite competing interpretations. History is argued, not merely reported.

**Bibliography as resource.** Comprehensive enough to serve as a research guide for any thread of the narrative.

## VII. Chapter Roles and Mathematical Depth

Not all chapters have the same balance of narrative, mathematics, and analysis. Different chapter types require different ratios of exposition.

### Chapter Role Categories

**Narrative-Driven Chapters** (Examples: 1, 2, 3, 11)
- **Purpose:** Establish context, introduce people and problems, set historical stakes
- **Narrative:** 55–60% of chapter content
- **Mathematics:** 15–20% (light derivations, mostly explanatory)
- **Analysis/Context:** 25–30%
- **Examples per chapter:** 1–2 worked examples, often brief
- **Diagrams per chapter:** 2–4
- **Guidance:** Include full exposition of major concepts but don't dwell on algebraic details. One thorough worked example per chapter is sufficient. Emphasize narrative flow over mathematical rigor.

**Technical-Method Chapters** (Examples: 4, 5, 6, 8, 9, 12, 13)
- **Purpose:** Develop a specific method, instrument, or mathematical technique
- **Narrative:** 25–30% (supporting context, human stakes)
- **Mathematics:** 50–60% (detailed derivations, multiple worked examples)
- **Analysis/Context:** 15–25%
- **Examples per chapter:** 3–5 worked examples with real data
- **Diagrams per chapter:** 4–8, often multi-step or detailed
- **Guidance:** Reader expects complete mathematical exposition. Include all six steps of the exposition sequence (from styleguide Part III) for each major concept. Don't abbreviate algebraic steps.

**Historiographical Chapters** (Examples: 7, 10, 18, 19)
- **Purpose:** Examine competing interpretations, evaluate evidence, discuss how historians have understood a period
- **Narrative:** 35–40% (historical events and contexts)
- **Mathematics:** 10–15% (minimal; illustrative only)
- **Analysis/Historiography:** 50–55% (scholarly debate, evidence evaluation, competing narratives)
- **Examples per chapter:** 0–2 brief worked examples if needed for context
- **Diagrams per chapter:** 1–3, mostly for clarification
- **Guidance:** Focus on engaging with historical scholarship. Present competing views neutrally, then offer nuanced assessment. Mathematics appears to support claims but isn't the chapter's primary focus. Dense citations to primary sources and historiographical scholarship.

**Synthesis/Overview Chapters** (Examples: 20, 25)
- **Purpose:** Draw together multiple threads, show long-term patterns, assess overall impact
- **Narrative:** 40–45%
- **Mathematics:** 5–10% (summary level only)
- **Analysis/Context:** 45–50%
- **Examples per chapter:** Brief summaries of earlier examples, no new detailed worked examples
- **Diagrams per chapter:** 1–3, comparative or summary in nature
- **Guidance:** Focus on synthesis and big-picture understanding. Reference earlier chapters with \cref{}. Avoid deep technical dives; instead, show connections and implications.

### Decision Framework for Authors

Before starting a chapter, ask:

1. **What is this chapter's primary purpose?** 
   - If establishing context or introducing problems: **Narrative-Driven**
   - If developing a technique or instrument: **Technical-Method**
   - If evaluating competing interpretations: **Historiographical**
   - If showing patterns across time: **Synthesis**

2. **How much mathematics will I need?**
   - Narrative-Driven: sketch and explain, light detail
   - Technical-Method: full exposition, algebraic steps, derivations
   - Historiographical: minimal; use mainly for context
   - Synthesis: very light; summative only

3. **How many worked examples do I need?**
   - Narrative-Driven: 1–2, brief
   - Technical-Method: 3–5, detailed, with real historical data
   - Historiographical: 0–2, only if directly relevant to argument
   - Synthesis: 0–1, summative

4. **What's my citation density?**
   - All chapters: dense, multiple per page
   - Technical-Method: especially dense in mathematics sections
   - Historiographical: very dense, cite all competing interpretations

## VIII. Historiography and Scholarly Debate

**Treat history as an argument.** History is not a sequence of facts that happened and then were recorded. It is a contested field where scholars interpret evidence differently. Your role as author is to acknowledge this reality and engage with it thoughtfully.

### When to Include Historiographical Perspective

Include historiographical discussion when:

- **Interpretations genuinely diverge.** Multiple credible scholars disagree on meaning or causation.
- **The disagreement illuminates the subject.** The debate reveals something about the period, the people, or the problem.
- **You have evidence to evaluate.** Don't describe debates you can't assess; instead, cite works that present the evidence.

Examples from Chapters 1–7:

- **Ch. 3 (Flamsteed's Funding):** How much financial hardship did Flamsteed really face? Was he the victim of royal neglect, or a man managing modest resources in an impoverished state? Sources differ; acknowledge both views and present evidence.

- **Ch. 7 (Board Historiography):** "Traditional narrative" (Harrison vs. obstructionist Board) versus "revisionist view" (Board's skepticism was reasonable given evidence). Present both, then offer nuanced assessment.

### Structural Pattern for Including Historiography

**Three-part movement:** Traditional view → Revisionist view → Synthesis

Example template:

For two centuries, historians described [subject] as [traditional interpretation]. [Citation for traditional view]. This narrative emphasized [what traditional view highlights].

More recent scholarship questions this account. [Revisionist historians] have argued that [alternative interpretation], citing [specific evidence]. [Citations for revisionist view]. This reinterpretation draws attention to [what revisionist view highlights].

A careful reading of [primary sources or careful scholars] suggests [nuanced synthesis]. [Specific evidence for synthesis]. The truth seems to lie in [how views complement or overlap].

### Timing in the Chapter

**Where does historiographical discussion belong?**

- **If the historiography is essential to the chapter's argument:** Include it mid-chapter, where it logically arises.
- **If the historiography is context but not essential:** Include it late in the section, after technical exposition.
- **If the historiography is tangential:** Defer to a footnote or back reference.

Example from Ch. 7: The historiography section came mid-chapter, *after* explaining the Board's composition and institutional context. This ordering let readers understand the facts before grappling with their interpretation.

### Cautions

- **Don't present historiography as trivia.** Include it only when it illuminates the subject.
- **Don't hide behind "some say."** Name the scholars or works; provide citations.
- **Don't assume the reader knows the debate.** Explain each view clearly and fairly.
- **Don't overstate disagreement.** Most scholarly disagreements are subtle refinements, not polar opposites.

## IX. Appendix Linking and Deferred Content

### Philosophy

Technical sufficiency in the main text; complete detail in appendices. Readers should be able to follow the main argument without consulting appendices, but appendices should provide everything for readers wanting deeper understanding.

### When to Defer Content to Appendix

**Defer to appendix if:**

- The content is essential for completeness but not essential for understanding the narrative.
- The content would interrupt the chapter flow if included inline.
- Multiple chapters reference the same detailed material (centralize it).
- The content is a lengthy worked example, full derivation, or historical document.

**Keep inline if:**

- The content is essential to the argument.
- Removing it would leave readers confused.
- It's brief enough not to disrupt flow (under 10 lines of text or one equation).

### Examples of Appendix Content

- **Full worked examples:** A chapter's data reduction could be expanded to an appendix showing all intermediate steps.
- **Lengthy derivations:** The full derivation of parallax formulas or nutation coefficients.
- **Primary source documents:** Full transcriptions of letters, excerpts from *Philosophical Transactions*, Board of Longitude minutes.
- **Historical data tables:** Extended comparison tables (all 50 stars vs. main text's 5).
- **Mathematical detail:** Proofs, alternative derivations, extension to 3D cases.
- **Instrument specifications:** Detailed mechanical drawings, material specifications, calibration procedures.

### Linking to Appendices Naturally

**Bad:** "See Appendix A for the full calculation."  
**Good:** "The complete reduction of this observation, showing all intermediate steps, appears in Appendix A; here we focus on the method and key steps."

**Pattern for natural appendix reference:**

[Main text describes method and gives key steps with one worked example]. Observers who wished to master the technique worked through complete examples like the one detailed in \cref{app:flamsteed-reduction}, where every step—clock correction, time conversion, refraction correction, coordinate transformation—is shown with actual numbers from Flamsteed's ledger.

This structure lets the reader stay engaged in the narrative while knowing where to find deeper detail.

### Appendix Organization

- **Appendix A–C:** Technical (derivations, worked examples, mathematical detail)  
- **Appendix D–E:** Historical documents (excerpts from primary sources, transcriptions)  
- **Appendix F–G:** Data and tables (extended reference tables, instrument specifications)  
- **Appendix H–J:** Biographical (brief biographies of key figures, if separate section is needed)  
- **Appendix K:** Bibliography (if separate from main bibliography)

## X. Figures and Diagrams: Use Them Liberally

**Use figures, diagrams, and tables plentifully.** They are not supplementary; they are primary evidence. Include:

- Astronomical diagrams (celestial sphere, stellar positions, orbital geometry)
- Instrument schematics with labeled components and dimensions
- Historical maps and observational records
- Data tables with actual historical measurements
- Mathematical plots illustrating relationships

**Captions must be substantive.** Never write "Figure 1: A diagram." Instead: "Figure 1: The celestial sphere as seen from latitude 51°N, showing hour angle ξ and declination δ. The observer measures altitude h and azimuth A at the meridian, yielding declination through $\delta = h + \text{refraction}(h)$."

**Use placeholder images during drafting.** Where you intend a figure but the final image is not ready, use:

```latex
\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures/placeholder.pdf}
  \caption{Flamsteed's Meridian Circle (1689): Schematic showing the fixed meridian axis, graduating scope, micrometer, and illumination system. [Placeholder: Replace with historical engraving or technical drawing.]}
  \label{fig:meridian-circle-schematic}
\end{figure}
```

Later, replace `placeholder.pdf` with the actual image and update the caption. The structure remains in place.

**Diagram precision supports mathematics.** If you derive an equation for aberration angle, provide a diagram showing the geometry: Earth's velocity vector **v**, incoming starlight direction **s**, the angle θ between them. Let the reader see the geometry that the mathematics describes.

**Small multiples and parallel structure.** When comparing instruments, methods, or observations across time, use consistent visual and verbal structure. Let the reader perceive patterns through juxtaposition.

**Layered information.** Present data at multiple resolutions: key results in text, detailed derivation in figures, raw data and source quotations in annotation.

## XI. Information Architecture

**Appendices.** Reserve for:
- Extended derivations that would interrupt narrative flow
- Complete worked examples with full numerical detail
- Instrument specifications and technical parameters
- Reference tables (constants, conversions, chronometer ratings)
- Biographical register

## XII. Prose and Tone

**Warm but precise.** Enthusiasm for an elegant mechanism or a clever proof can show—but earn it with rigor, not exclamation points.

**Active constructions.** "Flamsteed measured" not "measurements were made." Except where passive voice correctly emphasizes action over actor.

**Technical vocabulary without apology.** Use "collimation error," "escapement," "hour angle," "refrangibility" when they are the right terms. Define on first use; do not repeatedly gloss.

**The smallest effective difference.** When distinguishing concepts, use minimum necessary contrast. Don't overstate novelty or opposition. Let distinctions emerge through careful exposition.

**Occasional dry wit.** Welcome. Cleverness for its own sake is not.

---

## XIII. Avoid

- Anecdotes without analytical payoff
- Claims without citation
- Vague quantifiers ("very precise," "remarkably accurate")—give numbers
- Simplified diagrams that obscure actual geometry
- Analogies that mislead more than illuminate
- Mathematics banished to appendices when it belongs in the argument
- Rhetorical questions that condescend
- Clichés of science writing ("It turns out that...", "Little did he know...")
- Praising figures as geniuses; show their reasoning instead
- Excessive hedging or throat-clearing
- Exclamation points

## XIV. Literary Models and Hybrid Voice

This book synthesizes techniques from several master science writers. Think of it as a deliberate hybrid:

**Narrative Framing and Chapter Openings: Sobel and Holmes**
- *Dava Sobel* (*Longitude*, *Galileo's Daughter*): Opens chapters with vivid human moments that establish stakes before exposition. Creates intimate connection to historical actors without sentimentality. Each opening propels the reader forward with genuine curiosity about how the problem was solved.
- *Richard Holmes* (*The Age of Wonder*, *Simondon*): Weaves period texture throughout—sensory details, the texture of 18th-century life, how scientists thought and felt in their own moment. Treats figures as complex people with doubts, ambitions, and intellectual struggles visible in their work.
- *Your application*: Begin each chapter with a concrete scene—an observation, a failure, a decision—that establishes why this problem mattered. Let readers feel the urgency before understanding the solution.

**Mathematical Exposition: James Gleick**
- *Gleick* (*Chaos*, *The Information*, *Isaac Newton*): Explains difficult mathematics by building intuition first. Shows *why* the math matters before introducing formalism. Connects equations to the lived problem they solve. Maintains intellectual rigor while remaining accessible to educated generalists.
- *Your application*: Never present equations in isolation. Motivate each derivation with a concrete question or observation. Build geometric intuition before symbolic manipulation. Show how period astronomers actually computed results, sometimes with approximations. **Use multiple worked examples; don't shy from showing pages of calculation.**

**Instrument and Procedural Detail: Derek Howse (Elevated)**
- *Howse* (*Greenwich Time*, technical historiography): Treats instruments with respect as engineered solutions to real constraints. Explains mechanical principles, accuracy limits, practical workarounds. Never merely catalogs specifications—connects instruments to the specific observations they enabled.
- *Your application*: Describe instruments as if readers might operate or repair them. Explain not just what an arc measures, but why it's graduated at 5-minute intervals, how thermal expansion affects readings, what the observer actually does at the eyepiece. Make the reader feel the weight of brass, the scratching of the dividers, the cold of winter observation. **Include diagrams of key instruments.**

**Overall Tone: Winchester Tempered by Gleick**
- *Simon Winchester* (*Krakatoa*, *The Map That Changed the World*): Writes with infectious enthusiasm. Conveys genuine delight in discovery, in elegant solutions, in the intellectual drama of science. Never patronizes—assumes readers will share excitement about precision, methodology, and human ingenuity.
- *Gleick's counterbalance*: Tempers enthusiasm with intellectual seriousness. Acknowledges uncertainty and disagreement. Recognizes that progress is often messy, incremental, and built on earlier errors. Respects the difficulty of the problems these figures tackled.
- *Your application*: Write with warmth and genuine interest, but earn every expression of enthusiasm through rigorous exposition. Let readers feel your respect for the figures and problems, but also your critical distance. Avoid gushing; let the elegance of a solution speak for itself.
**The Unified Voice**
Imagine: Gleick's intellectual ambition and rigor as the foundation. Sobel's accessibility and narrative momentum in chapter structure. Holmes's period texture and human complexity throughout. Winchester's enthusiasm held in check by Gleick's seriousness. Your own judgment about which stories matter most and how they connect. The result is a book that demands intelligence from readers but never condescends to them—that finds the human drama in technical history without sacrificing precision.

## XVI. Writing a Chapter

### Step 1: Set Up the Prompt

When prompting an LLM to write a chapter, include in this order:

```
1. Relevant section from this global guide
2. The specific chapter prompt (from prompts/chapter_prompts.md)
3. The specific chapter outline (from prompts/chapter_outlines.md)
4. Any special instructions (see below)
```

### Step 2: Update Cross-References

Each chapter must maintain consistency with the document structure:

**Table of Contents**
- The `\chapter{}` command automatically updates the TOC
- Use `\section{}` and `\subsection{}` consistently

**List of Figures**
- Every `\caption{}` in a figure environment automatically appears in LOF
- Ensure captions are substantive (what the figure shows AND why it matters)

**List of Tables**
- Every `\caption{}` in a table environment automatically appears in LOT
- Captions should describe content and significance

**Cross-References Within Text**
```latex
As we saw in Chapter~\ref{ch:founding} (p.~\pageref{ch:founding}), Flamsteed's funding was inadequate.

The derivation appears in Appendix~\ref{app:spherical-trig}.

See Figure~\ref{fig:transit-circle} for the instrument layout.
```

### Step 3: Update Appendices

After writing a chapter, check if any appendix needs updating:

| If the chapter contains... | Update this appendix |
|---------------------------|---------------------|
| New mathematical derivation referenced | Appendix A: Mathematical Derivations |
| New instrument discussed with specifications | Appendix B: Instrument Specifications |
| New Astronomer Royal mentioned substantially | Appendix C: The Astronomers Royal |
| New location at Greenwich described | Appendix D: Visiting Greenwich |
| New technical term introduced | Appendix E: Glossary |
| New sources cited | Appendix F: Bibliography |

**Example: Adding to Appendix A**

If Chapter 12 references the aberration derivation:
```latex
% In Chapter 12:
The full derivation of the aberration formula appears in Appendix~\ref{app:aberration}.

% In Appendix A, section A.3:
\section{Stellar Aberration}
\label{app:aberration}
[Full derivation as specified in appendix outline]
```

**Example: Adding to Appendix B**

If Chapter 4 discusses Flamsteed's mural arc:
```latex
% Add to the Meridian Instruments table in Appendix B:
Flamsteed's mural arc & 1689 & Sharp & -- & 140° arc & 10--15'' & Greenwich & Flamsteed's principal instrument \\
```

**Example: Adding to Appendix E (Glossary)**

If Chapter 8 introduces "clearing the distance":
```latex
\item[Clearing the distance] The process of correcting an observed lunar distance for the effects of parallax and atmospheric refraction to obtain the true geocentric distance. See Chapter~\ref{ch:lunar-distance}.
```

## XIX. Sample Passage Architecture

A section on stellar aberration might proceed:

1. *Narrative hook:* Bradley at Kew in December 1725, attempting to measure stellar parallax with Molyneux's zenith sector, puzzled by a displacement in the wrong direction.

2. *Problem statement:* The expected parallax ellipse versus the observed annual shift. Why the geometry didn't match.

3. *Diagram:* Earth's orbital velocity vector, incoming starlight, the tilted telescope. [Figure with clear labels showing geometric relationships.]

4. *Physical insight:* The analogy Bradley reportedly drew—raindrops falling into a moving carriage.

5. *Mathematical derivation:* The aberration angle as $\alpha \approx v/c$, the geometry of velocity addition. Show step-by-step; don't skip algebraic steps.

6. *Worked example:* Bradley's observations of γ Draconis: actual measured displacements from his logs (with citation), the implied speed of light, comparison to Rømer's earlier estimate.

7. *Error analysis:* Limits of the zenith sector, atmospheric refraction effects, the precision of Bradley's clock. Quantify each error source.

8. *Historical context and citations:* Bradley's 1729 paper in *Philosophical Transactions*, contemporary reactions, how this confirmed Copernican theory, implications for stellar distance measurement. Dense citation throughout.

9. *Implications and connection:* What this result meant for observational astronomy, how it was used by later astronomers. Set up the next chapter's theme.

8. *Implications:* Confirmation of Earth's motion, a new upper bound on stellar distances (parallax still undetected), an independent check on Rømer's speed of light.

9. *Sidenotes throughout:* Citations to Bradley's 1729 paper in *Philosophical Transactions*, cross-reference to the later discovery of nutation, biographical note on Molyneux.

This integration—narrative, mathematics, data, citation, human consequence—is the signature of the book.
